#### le chatelier's principle

when a chemical system at equilibrium is disturbed, the system shifts in the direction that reduces this effect

##### a change in _concentration_ of reactants/products will change the equilibrium and prob turn into Q

        * an increase in the reactants will cause a reaction to shift right for Q<K and generate more products

        * an increase in the products will cause a reaction to shift left for Q>K to generate more reactants

        * a decrease in either will cause the inverse reaction

        * increasing the amount of rando reactants/products that r not in the proper state phase will not have an effect

once equilibrium is re-established, the concentrations will be different but the K will be the same

##### changes in _pressure_ affect equilibrium systems as well

        * increasing pressure (decreasing volume) = shift towards *fewer* moles of gas

        * decreasing pressure (increasing volume) = shift towards *more* moles of gas

        * equal gas moles on both sides -> no shift

        * adding an inert gas at constant volume -> no effect

ex.

if you reduce the partial pressure of a product, you are losing product, and we need to shift right to replenish what's lost for equilibrium
thus, you need to consume more reactant to reestablish equilibrium :)

##### changes in _temperature_

when heat is added to a system by increasing temperature, equilibrium shifts to undo the effect

the van't Hoff equation shows this relationship

        for exothermic reactions where heat is a product
        * increase in temp -> shift left, K decreases
        * decrease in temp -> shift right, K increases

for a reaction with delta H < 0 , we will treat heat as a product

        for endothermic reactions where heat is a reactant
        * increase in temp -> shift right, K increases
        * decrease in temp -> shift left, K decreases

for a reaction with delta H > 0 , we will treat heat as a reactant

##### the lack of effect of a catalyst

these affect the kinetics but not the equilibrium

it can speed/slow the reaction, but not change anything that actually occurs during equilibrium
